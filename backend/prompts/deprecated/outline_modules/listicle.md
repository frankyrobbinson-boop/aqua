Listicle structure (outline stage). The title promises a countable set of things; deliver exactly those, each as a turn.

- **Plan exactly {item_count} sections - one per item.** One item = one section. Never combine items.
- **No preamble section and no summary/playbook section.** Section 1 is item 1; the last section is the last item. Don't bolt a recap or theory section onto either end.
- **{item_count} items, one promise.** The items are not {item_count} separate topics - each one is another way the title's promise comes true. Whatever an item teaches, its payoff lands in the promise's currency, and the list is ordered so the promise compounds - more true with each item. If every item carries its own separate problem and its own "go do this," the video becomes {item_count} errands, and {item_count} errands is a chore.
- **The act lives only in the close, on the peak.** Do not let any individual item carry the act. The single thing the viewer goes and does is the peak item - the one the hook's bookmark points at and withholds - paid off in the conclusion. The other items are why that one act is worth standing up for.
- **Each item is a turn, not an entry.** For each section plan all four beats: the belief, failure, or want it opens on; the counterintuitive turn + its one-phrase mechanism; the payoff in the promise's currency; and the single FEELING it delivers. Name the feeling in `purpose`. No two adjacent sections should deliver the same feeling.
- **Find each item's one feeling.** This is the work. The same fact dressed as a spec is dead; dressed as a feeling it's a section. Every item has one.
- **Plan the stitches.** When two items share ground - the same rule inverted, the same tool, the same spot in the yard - note it in `purpose` so the script can reference one from the other. Cross-references are what make {item_count} items feel like one video.
- **Order for retention.** Open with a strong item. Put your single best item - the highest feeling, usually the peak/act item - second OR last for maximum pull; bury the weakest in the middle. The bookmarked peak can sit deep in the list as the withheld payoff.
- **Mark the peak.** In `purpose`, mark the one or two highest-feeling sections as "peak." At least two sections should be flagged as escalation - the ones the script signals as the big ones. The hook's numbered bookmark must point at the peak that carries the act.
- **Teach repeated procedure once.** If an install/care step repeats across every item, name in `key_material` which single section teaches it in full. Do not plan the full procedure into all {item_count} sections.
- **Hook names no items.** The hook promises the count and the stakes and points its bookmark at a peak - it never lists the items. Each reveal belongs to its own section.

- **`section.title` is the literal item name, nothing else.** Plain item identifier — the plant, the tool, the rule, the mistake. No micro-thesis, no teaser, no feeling-in-the-title (the outline_base.md "micro-thesis" rule is overridden for listicles; for a listicle the section's surprise lives in `purpose` and the script opening, not the title). This is so the script, the visuals stage, and the assembler can all key off the same string. The general "micro-thesis title" guidance from outline_base.md applies to non-listicle structures.
