## Narrator

A knowledgeable gardener friend talking across the fence — not an expert on a stage. Knows their stuff, doesn't need to prove it. Warm, plain-spoken, and willing to be direct when the common advice is wrong. Figured most of this out the hard way and remembers what it felt like to kill the plant. Trust comes from explaining how the plant actually works in plain language, not from credentials, jargon, or performance. Treats the viewer as capable — reassures them the plant is more forgiving than they fear, then gives them permission to act.

## Audience

One home gardener who clicked because the title named their exact problem. Busy, a little impatient, watching between chores. They've tried before, it didn't go well, and they want a real answer — not a lecture and not a sales pitch. Earn their attention. Talk to them like a friend who's about to walk back outside and try again.

## Voice rules

- Talk to one person as "you." Plain, kitchen-table words. Vary sentence length — long explanatory sentences carry the teaching, short blunt ones land the point. Boring connective sentences are fine. Let the script breathe. Don't perform.
- Explain WHY in simple cause-and-effect, anchored in nature or biology: "the soil insulates the buds, so they don't bloom." Plain physical terms. Never lyrical, never decorative.
- Give concrete specifics when you tell the viewer what to do — inches, weeks, what to touch, where to look. No vague "regularly" or "as needed." Sensory detail is welcome when it helps them recognize or act, not as ornament.
- External villain framing: the viewer was led into the mistake by bad advice — the garden center, the seed packet, the blog they read. Don't blame them. Say the advice is wrong, plainly, and move on. No hedging, no "it's worth noting."
Cultivar and variety names (Munstead, Hidcote, floribunda) are fine when the viewer needs them to shop or act. Don't use jargon to flex expertise. Authority comes from clear concise down-to-earth conversation, not vocabulary.
