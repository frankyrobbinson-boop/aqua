# Orientation (who this script speaks to and how it positions them)

- **Promise targets a pain.** Speak to a viewer in a felt pain or stuck situation — never to one already winning. "Why your X stops doing Y" beats "How to make X do Y better." Frame the script as rescue or escape, not optimization.
- **External villain — vague, not named — and absolution before instruction.** Within the first ~75 words, blame must be explicitly lifted off the viewer and redirected to an outside antagonist — an industry that profits from their ignorance, conventional wisdom that's measurably wrong, an assumption that's been sold to them (e.g. "free = worthless"). Use the absolution move directly: "X doesn't die because you're a bad gardener — it dies because almost everything you've been told is wrong." Keep the antagonist *faceless*: "what they tell you," "every blog," "the advice that gets repeated everywhere" — not a named competitor, brand, or expert; a vague "they" can't push back. Add a second-layer physical antagonist the body teaches the viewer to recognize (rot, root-cooking, defoliation, leaching) — the advice-villain explains why the viewer was misled; the physical-villain gives them an enemy they can see. The viewer is never the source of the problem. Mistakes are something the viewer was *led into*. Grammar of inherited error: "someone told you," "you've probably heard," "the instinct is." Banned framings: "your mistake," "you keep doing," "you're getting it wrong."
- **Subject as protagonist with strategic intent.** Whatever the video is about — a plant, a habit, a tool, a market — give it agency, and specifically give it a survival agenda or biological goal ("a rose has exactly one goal in life — reproduce," "the plant thinks mission accomplished and pours its energy into seeds"). It wants, refuses, hides, rewards, demands. The viewer's job becomes reading what it wants, not executing steps on it. Turns instruction into a relationship and mechanism explanation into character motivation. (Channel voice rules govern tone — adult motives, not cute mascots.)
- **Identity-conferring close.** The conclusion hands the viewer a new identity ("the kind of gardener who keeps the old knowledge alive," "the people who never buy X again") — not a padded prose recap of techniques (a short, rhythmic compressed-recap drumroll is allowed and specified in the Conclusion structure below) and not a polite sign-off.

# Universal rules (every channel, every video type obeys these)

- **Voice rules govern how you speak; structure rules govern how you organize.** 
- **Earn every sentence.** Each sentence either reveals information, drives a feeling, opens or closes a curiosity loop, or signals an imminent payoff (must be paid off in the next sentence or two).
- **Label the payoff before you deliver it.** Inside a segment, when you're about to land the most useful sentence, flag it first with an anticipation marker — "here's the part nobody mentions," "here's the rule that actually works," "and this is the part people skip" — then deliver. Use this at least once per segment. Don't deliver the secret naked; the label is what makes the viewer lean in.
- **Fragments are weapons.** Break long sentences with deliberate 2–5 word fragments that land like percussion. Use them to slam a single idea, contradict the prior sentence, or punctuate a reveal. Examples of the shape: "Don't lie." "Almost entirely." "Be decisive." "Catastrophically wrong." At least one such fragment per segment.
- **Rule of three.** Stack parallel clauses or fragments in groups of three for any key beat — escalating in weight, not just repeating. The third beat should sharpen or invert the first two. Shapes: three parallel diagnoses ("The watering advice is wrong. The soil advice is wrong. The pruning advice? Catastrophically wrong."), three stacked sensory phrases ("baked by sun, battered by wind, and ignored by rain"), or three one-word adjectives that name a rhythm ("Deep, lazy, low."). Use at least twice per script — once in the hook block, at least once mid-body. Triadic structures often resolve in a fragment — the compound move is more powerful than either alone.
- **Transitions are open loops that escalate stakes.** Each segment ends by naming what's at stake in the next segment as a revelation, an escalation, or a turn against the shared antagonist — never as logistics. Banned patterns: "next, we'll talk about…", "now let's move on to…", "another thing to consider is…". Required shape: the transition either (a) reveals that the next idea undoes or outranks this one ("this one feeds the plant — but the next doesn't feed the plant at all"), (b) raises the previous section's stake higher ("now that your soil is rich, let's talk about feeding — because timing matters as much as the fertilizer itself"), or (c) declares the next section the master key ("here's the final secret, and it's the one that determines whether any of the previous four even matter").
- **Tension thread must carry, AND a thematic echo phrase must recur.** (a) The antagonist installed in the hook resurfaces in at least 2 mid-body segment transitions and pays off explicitly near the end. (b) Hook promises must be paid off out loud: if the hook forward-referenced a numbered item or made any "stick with me, because…" promise, the script MUST cash it with an explicit callback line at the moment of delivery — "here's the one I promised you," "this is the secret I told you to stay for." Silent payoff is a broken promise. (c) Separately, install ONE signature image, metaphor, or refrain in the hook or first section and restate it — same exact phrasing or a tight variation — in at least two later sections and once in the conclusion ("a Mediterranean hillside in your backyard," "rhythm beats randomness"). Pick the echo from the channel's voice or the topic's central image.
- **Punctuation is breath, not grammar.** TTS reads this verbatim, so write for the ear. Use em-dashes for the speaker's mid-thought turn or aside ("The pruning advice? Catastrophically wrong."). Use stacked commas for accelerating lists that mimic a speaker piling on detail ("The wilting, the yellowing lower leaves, the sad gray cast that makes you reach for the hose — those are signs of overwatering."). Use question-mark mid-sentence to flag a single word the speaker is challenging. Use periods to slow down and land. Write numbers the way they should be spoken.
- **Ground every claim in the research.** Don't invent facts, names, or numbers.
- **Folksy quantifiers.** Exception: *rhetorical quantifiers* ("nine times out of ten," "ninety percent of buyers," "almost nobody gets this right") are allowed and encouraged when summarizing a real pattern — they do the work of a citation by implying long observation, without claiming a study. Use them sparingly, at moments where you're naming a common failure or widespread mistake. Do not present them as measured statistics.
- **Insider-status flip.** At least twice in the body, lift the viewer above a faceless out-group — "ninety percent of buyers," "the people who panic-prune," "most beginners," "everyone who walks into the nursery without this." Knowing what they now know puts them ahead of that group. Status, not just information, is the reward for watching.
- **The mechanism IS the proof.** When you make a claim — especially a counterintuitive one — earn it by explaining *why* it works in physical, biological, or causal terms the viewer can feel. Never lean on "studies show" or "experts say" as a substitute. A vivid one-sentence mechanism ("the essential oils concentrate when the plant is stressed," "a defoliated rose cannot flower because blooming is solar-powered") does more work than any citation. If you can't explain the why, cut the claim.
- **Do not write in the citation register.** No researcher names, no university affiliations, no "a 2019 study," no "according to the USDA," no journal references. Authority comes from mechanism, origin, domain vocabulary, calibrated specifics, and confident declaratives. The one exception: a named figure may appear when the research has a real, surprising story attached.
- **Saturate with second person.** "You" / "your" / imperatives aimed at the viewer must appear in every paragraph. The viewer is the grammatical subject of the script; third-person plant-biology or industry-history mode is allowed only in short bursts (2–3 sentences) before the script snaps back to "so what you do is," "stick your finger in," "walk your garden." If a paragraph has no "you," rewrite it.
- **Permission to do less.** At least once in the body, the rescue must be "stop doing something," not "add another step." Tell the viewer to stop watering, stop feeding, stop fussing, stop forcing — and frame the stop as relief, not a new chore. Prefer "stop doing X" over "start doing Y" when correcting a mistake. For an exhausted viewer, permission to put a chore down is a felt reward; another to-do is friction.
- **Turn to the viewer mid-script.** At least twice in the body, break the lecture and address the viewer as if mid-conversation — anticipate their reaction before they have it ("I know that sounds insane," "if you've ever done X, raise your hand," "are you ready?", "if I could fix one habit, it would be this"). Validates resistance before it becomes a reason to click away. Do not overuse — these land because they're rare.
- **Paint the stuck-state, don't describe the problem.** When naming a problem, render the exact tableau the viewer is currently living inside — the dead twigs, the leggy stick, the three brown leaves, the jar that never sold. Specific enough that the viewer thinks "that's literally mine." Generic descriptions ("your plant struggles," "things go wrong") are banned; show the failure, don't label it.
- **Pictures beat diagnoses.** When describing a problem, result, or state, give the viewer the physical image first; the label (overwatered, root-bound, leggy, deficient) is optional and comes after. "A woody, hollow, dead-looking donut with green only at the tips" before "that's lavender that was pruned into old wood." Abstract status words alone ("healthy," "stressed," "happy") are not enough — pair them with a tableau or cut them.
- **Verbs do the work.** Choose kinetic, physical verbs over neutral ones — drown, smother, butcher, sabotage, hijack, pump, flood, starve, choke, cook, strangle. The verb should carry the emotion so adverbs become unnecessary. "You're smothering a desert survivor" beats "you're watering it too much." Audit every sentence: if the verb is to-be, to-have, to-do, or to-get, replace it unless the meaning truly demands it.
- **Talk with the viewer, not at them.** Use second-person imperatives and rhetorical questions to break the fourth wall and turn passive listening into call-and-response. At least one direct command per segment ("Stick your finger in the soil. If you feel any moisture at all, walk away.") and at least one rhetorical question per segment that the viewer is implicitly nodding to. Address one person, not a crowd — "you," never "you guys" or "everyone."
- **One reframing metaphor per segment.** Find a single arresting metaphor that flips how the viewer mentally categorizes the subject ("lavender is a tough Mediterranean weed wearing a pretty dress," "roses are solar-powered machines," "fresh stems are flower factories"). The metaphor isn't decoration — it does the conceptual work of the whole segment, so the rest of the segment can lean on it. Use one strong metaphor per segment, not three weak ones. If you can't find one that genuinely reframes, skip rather than reach.
- **Translate jargon in the same breath.** When a technical term, Latin name, cultivar, or specialist label appears, attach a plain-language tag immediately — never let the term stand alone. The pattern is term + comma + humanizing description: "the stoechas varieties, the funny little rabbit-ear ones," "lavandin, the big robust workhorses you see in commercial fields," "floribunda, the roses literally engineered to flower wave after wave." Signals expertise without alienating a beginner. If you can't translate it, don't use it.
- **Speak in declaratives.** Certainty is the credential. Ban hedging: no "might," "maybe," "sometimes," "in my opinion," "studies suggest," "it's generally thought." Counterintuitive claims must land as established fact — "A defoliated rose cannot flower. Period." "Lavender wood does not regenerate." "In winter, you don't water at all. None." If a claim isn't defensible enough to assert flatly, cut it.
- **Concentrate specifics at the moments that matter.** Authority isn't built by spraying numbers everywhere — it's built by placing one calibrated specific at each high-leverage moment: an exact depth ("two inches down"), a time window ("two to three weeks"), a count ("six hours of direct sun"), a ratio ("twice as wide as the root ball but only as deep"). One precise number wrapped in plain language reads as tested and lived-in. Every section should land at least one such specific where the action happens; never invent one — pull from research or omit.
- **Origin-as-authority beats credential-as-authority.** When the topic has a natural, traditional, or original context (where it grew up, how it was historically done, what it evolved to do), invoke *that* as the ground truth instead of a researcher's name. "How it actually works in the wild" is more persuasive in this register than "a 2019 Cornell study found."
- **Diagnostic checklist.** Where possible, give the viewer a one-sentence diagnostic they can run on their own situation today — finger-in-soil test, count the hours of sun, look for X color/pattern. Recognition tasks are more engaging than abstract knowledge; the viewer pauses the video to go check.

# Universal structure

- **Hook:** The hook (~200–300 words, may run as one flowing paragraph or several) must contain ALL of these elements; order and length are dictated by rhythm, not by a fixed beat count: (a) Pattern interrupt — a private failure the viewer recognizes and refuses to let them hide from ("you've killed X before — don't lie"); (b) Felt-pain tableau — the exact stuck-state the viewer is living inside, rendered in second person; (c) External-villain absolution — blame lifted off the viewer in the same breath as the villain is named ("X doesn't die because you're a bad gardener; it dies because almost everything you've been told is wrong"); (d) Triadic indictment of the bad advice (optional but high-leverage, especially for counter_claim/myth_bust); (e) Withheld promise — count and stakes, never the items themselves; (f) Numbered curiosity bookmark — name ONE later section by its number ("stick with me, because secret #N is the one almost no one gets right"); (g) Front-loaded end-state promise — "by the end of this video, you'll…" followed by 2–3 concrete capabilities, ideally using the triadic "exactly…exactly…exactly" rhythm; (h) Transition thread / open-loop into segment 1.

**Blend, don't pick one.** The archetype above is your *primary* opening move — but the five archetypes (scene, contrast, counter_claim, specific_result, bold_claim) are tools, not mutually exclusive categories. The strongest hooks layer 2–3 of them: a counter_claim landed inside a scene, a bold_claim that resolves into a specific_result, a contrast that sharpens into a counter_claim. Execute the primary archetype as your dominant frame, then weave in at least one secondary archetype move inside the same hook. Single-archetype hooks read thin.

- **Main segments:** one per outline section, averaging ~{words_per_segment} words but **not uniform**. Peak segments (the 1–2 marked as peak inside the outline's purpose text / bookmarked in the hook) run 1.4×–1.8× the average word count and carry more sub-beats — extra sensory specifics, an extra mechanism step, a warning, or a small story. Supporting segments run lean, closer to 0.7×–0.9× the average. Cumulative total still hits ~{total_word_target} words. Peaks and valleys, not a metronome.

Peak markers (1–2 per video, never more): exactly one or two segments — the genuine highest-leverage ideas, usually the section the hook bookmarked plus at most one other — must open with a verbal escalation marker that flags "this matters most": "this is the big one," "if you take nothing else from this video," "if I could fix one habit for every beginner, it would be this one," "here's the secret I promised you." Use the marker only where content earns it; using it on every section neutralizes the device.

Each segment needs a `visual_notes`: one short line on mood and subject.
- **Conclusion (~120–180 words).** Three rhythmic beats in this order: (1) Compressed-recap drumroll — a single rhythmic sentence or short fragment chain that sprints through every section in parallel imperatives (3–7 words per beat, one beat per section): "Treat it like a weed, not a pet. Build it a hillside, not a flowerbed. Water it almost never..." This is a percussive finale, not a paragraph of recap. (2) Single-action prescriptive close paired with permission-not-to-do-the-rest — "Don't try to do all ten at once. Pick the one that hit you hardest — the one where you went 'oh, that's what I've been doing wrong' — and fix just that this week." (3) Identity-conferring beat + villain callback — a future-tense picture of who the viewer now is ("the kind of gardener whose plants are so resilient you could forget about them for a month"). Land the final beat on an aphorism — one compact, quotable line. A warm sign-off after the identity beat is fine ("Until next time…"); the ban is on polite *replacements* for the identity beat, not on bookend phrasing. The CTA goes in the separate `cta` field.
