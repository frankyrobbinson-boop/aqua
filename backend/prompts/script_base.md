# Orientation (who this script speaks to and how it positions them)

- **Promise targets a pain.** Speak to a viewer in a felt pain or stuck situation — never to one already winning. "Why your X stops doing Y" beats "How to make X do Y better." Frame as rescue, not optimization.
- **External villain — vague, not named — and absolution before instruction.** Within the first ~75 words, blame must be lifted off the viewer and redirected to an outside antagonist (industry profiting from ignorance, conventional wisdom that's measurably wrong). Keep the antagonist *faceless*: "what they tell you," "every blog" — not a named competitor. Mistakes are something the viewer was *led into*. Banned framings: "your mistake," "you keep doing," "you're getting it wrong."
- **Subject as protagonist with strategic intent.** Give the subject agency and a survival agenda ("a rose has exactly one goal in life — reproduce"). The viewer reads what it wants, not executes steps on it.
- **Identity-conferring close.** The conclusion hands the viewer a new identity — not a padded prose recap and not a polite sign-off. (Short rhythmic drumroll specified in Conclusion below.)

# Universal rules (every channel, every video type obeys these)

- **Voice rules govern how you speak; structure rules govern how you organize.**
- **Earn every sentence.** Each sentence reveals information, drives a feeling, opens or closes a curiosity loop, or signals an imminent payoff (paid off in the next sentence or two).
- **Ground every claim in the research.** Don't invent facts, names, or numbers.
- **Fragments are weapons.** Break long sentences with deliberate 2–5 word fragments that land like percussion — slam an idea, contradict, or punctuate a reveal. "Catastrophically wrong." At least one per segment.
- **Rule of three.** Stack parallel clauses or fragments in groups of three for any key beat, escalating in weight — the third beat should sharpen or invert the first two. Example: "The watering advice is wrong. The soil advice is wrong. The pruning advice? Catastrophically wrong." Use at least twice per script (once in hook, once mid-body). NEVER at a section closer — beat 6c bans the triple-fragment closer; that pattern has become a template.
- **Transitions are open loops — no spoken section numbers.** Each segment ends by naming what's at stake in the next as revelation, escalation, or turn against the antagonist — never logistics. Banned: "next, we'll talk about…", "now let's move on to…", and any spoken section number. The ONE exception is the numbered cliffhanger inside the hook and its single payoff callback when that section opens. Three shapes — (a) undo: next idea outranks this one; (b) escalate: raises previous section's stake; (c) master-key: next section determines whether any of the previous matter.
- **Tension thread + echo phrase.** (a) Antagonist installed in the hook resurfaces in 2+ mid-body transitions and pays off explicitly near the end. (b) Hook promises pay off out loud: any "stick with me, because…" promise MUST cash with an explicit callback ("here's the one I promised you"). Silent payoff is a broken promise. (c) Install ONE signature image, metaphor, or refrain in the hook or first section; restate it — same phrasing or tight variation — in 2+ later sections and once in the conclusion.
- **Punctuation is breath, not grammar.** TTS reads this verbatim — write for the ear. Em-dashes for mid-thought turns, stacked commas for piling on, mid-sentence question-marks to challenge a single word, periods to land. Example: "The wilting, the yellowing lower leaves, the sad gray cast — those are signs of overwatering." Write numbers the way they should be spoken.
- **Folksy quantifiers.** Rhetorical quantifiers ("nine times out of ten") are allowed when summarizing a real pattern. Use sparingly at moments naming a common failure.
- **Insider-status flip.** At least twice in the body, lift the viewer above a faceless out-group ("most beginners"). Status, not just information, is the reward for watching.
- **Mechanism over citation.** Three angles on one rule:
  - *Mechanism IS the proof.* Earn counterintuitive claims by explaining *why* in physical, biological, or causal terms the viewer can feel. A vivid one-sentence mechanism ("a defoliated rose cannot flower because blooming is solar-powered") does more work than any citation. If you can't explain the why, cut the claim.
  - *No citation register.* No researcher names, no university affiliations, no "a 2019 study," no "according to the USDA." Authority comes from mechanism, origin, domain vocabulary, calibrated specifics, and confident declaratives. One exception: a named figure may appear when research has a real, surprising story attached.
  - *Origin-as-authority beats credential-as-authority.* Invoke where it grew up, how it was historically done — not a researcher's name.
- **Saturate with second person.** "You" / "your" / imperatives in every paragraph. The viewer is the grammatical subject; third-person mode allowed only in 2–3 sentence bursts before snapping back.
- **Permission to do less.** At least once in the body, the rescue must be "stop doing something," not "add another step." Prefer "stop doing X" over "start doing Y."
- **Turn to the viewer mid-script.** At least twice in the body, address the viewer as if mid-conversation ("I know that sounds insane"). Don't overuse — these land because they're rare.
- **Pictures, not labels.** When naming a problem, render the exact tableau the viewer is living inside — the dead twigs, the leggy stick, the three brown leaves. The label (overwatered, root-bound) is optional and comes after. Generic descriptions ("your plant struggles") and abstract status words alone ("healthy," "stressed") are banned — pair with a tableau or cut.
- **Verbs do the work.** Kinetic, physical verbs over neutral ones — drown, smother, butcher, sabotage, hijack, flood, starve, choke, cook. The verb carries the emotion. "You're smothering a desert survivor" beats "you're watering it too much."
- **Trust your metaphor — don't pre-negate it.** Lead with the image; contrast is implied. Ban the "You're not X. You're Y." antithesis frame. Banned: "You're not planting a to-do list. You're planting a pension in the ground." Land: "You're planting a pension in the ground."
- **Talk with the viewer, not at them.** One direct command + one rhetorical question per segment. Address one person — never "you guys."
- **One reframing metaphor per segment.** A single arresting metaphor that flips how the viewer categorizes the subject. It does the conceptual work of the segment. One strong, not three weak. If you can't find one that genuinely reframes, skip rather than reach.
- **Translate jargon in the same breath — vary the syntax.** When a technical term, Latin name, or cultivar appears, attach a plain-language tag immediately. **Never use the same translation shape twice in a row.** Four sanctioned shapes: (1) em-dash/comma appositive; (2) separate sentence; (3) embedded relative clause; (4) parenthetical aside. If you can't translate it, don't use it.
- **Speak in declaratives.** Certainty is the credential. Ban hedging: no "might," "maybe," "sometimes," "in my opinion," "studies suggest." Counterintuitive claims land as fact — "In winter, you don't water at all. None." If a claim isn't defensible enough to assert flatly, cut it.
- **Concentrate specifics at the moments that matter.** One calibrated specific at each high-leverage moment — an exact depth ("two inches down"), a time window ("two to three weeks"). Every section lands at least one such specific where the action happens; pull from research or omit.
- **Diagnostic checklist.** Where possible, give a one-sentence diagnostic the viewer can run today — finger-in-soil test, count the hours of sun. Recognition tasks pause the video.

# Universal structure

- **Hook (~200–300 words, may run as one flowing paragraph or several)** must contain ALL eight elements: (a) Pattern interrupt — private failure the viewer recognizes ("you've killed X before — don't lie"); (b) Felt-pain tableau in second person; (c) External-villain absolution in the same breath as the villain is named; (d) Triadic indictment of the bad advice (optional, high-leverage for counter_claim/myth_bust); (e) Withheld promise — count and stakes, never items; (f) Numbered curiosity bookmark — name ONE later section by its number ("stick with me, because secret #N is the one almost no one gets right"); (g) Front-loaded end-state promise — "by the end of this video, you'll…" with 2–3 concrete capabilities, ideally triadic "exactly…exactly…exactly" rhythm; (h) Transition / open-loop into segment 1.

**Blend, don't pick one.** The archetype is your primary frame. Layer at least one secondary archetype move; single-archetype hooks read thin.

- **Main segments:** one per outline section, averaging ~{words_per_segment} words but **not uniform**. Peak segments (1–2 marked as peak / bookmarked in the hook) run 1.4×–1.8× the average and carry more sub-beats — extra sensory specifics, mechanism step, warning, or small story. Supporting segments run lean, 0.7×–0.9× the average. Cumulative total hits ~{total_word_target} words. Peaks and valleys, not a metronome.

Peak markers (1–2 per video, never more): one or two segments open with a verbal escalation marker that flags "this matters most" — e.g. "if I could fix one habit for every beginner, it would be this one." Using the marker on every section neutralizes the device.

Each segment needs a `visual_notes`: one short line on mood and subject.

- **Conclusion (~120–180 words).** Three rhythmic beats in this order:
  1. **Compressed-recap drumroll** — a single rhythmic sentence or short fragment chain that sprints through every section in parallel imperatives (3–7 words per beat, one beat per section). Percussive finale, not a paragraph of recap.
  2. **Single-action prescriptive close** paired with permission-not-to-do-the-rest — tell the viewer to pick the ONE that hit hardest and fix just that this week.
  3. **Identity-conferring beat + villain callback** — a future-tense picture of who the viewer now is. Land the final beat on an aphorism — one compact, quotable line. A warm sign-off after the identity beat is fine ("Until next time…"); the ban is on polite *replacements* for the identity beat. The CTA goes in the separate `cta` field.
