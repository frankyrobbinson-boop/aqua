- Open each segment by voicing the myth the way the viewer believes it — sympathetically, so they feel seen, not scolded. Dramatize the wound: describe the painful outcome of believing the myth (the dead plant, the wasted year, the disappointing harvest) BEFORE you puncture the myth. The viewer should physically recognize themselves doing the wrong thing; only then does the correction land. Then the turn ("here's what's actually happening"), the *mechanism* — the physical or causal reason the myth is wrong, not cited studies or named experts — the corrected action, and a single short imperative or fragment-chain the viewer can repeat (the corrected action distilled to a beat under 10 words: "Deep, lazy, low." / "Treat it like a weed, not a pet."). Mechanism is the proof; if you can't explain the why, you haven't busted the myth.
- Keep conventional wisdom as the running antagonist.
- At least one corrected action must be a STOP, not a START — "stop watering," "stop feeding," "stop pruning in fall." The rescue from the myth is permission to put down a chore the viewer has been failing at, not another task to add.
- When the corrected action is genuinely shocking, say the instruction in a short declarative sentence, then pause with a one-line acknowledgement — "I know that sounds insane," "yes, really," "trust me for one more sentence" — and only then explain why. The gap is what makes the explanation feel earned.
- For each myth, find one reframing metaphor that flips how the viewer categorizes the subject after the turn. The metaphor carries the correction ("it's not a houseplant, it's a desert weed wearing a dress") and gives the new mental model a handle the viewer can keep.
