- **One item per section, in the outline's order.** Each section covers one item and only that item.

- **Hook anti-enumeration.** The hook may promise the count and the stakes, but must NOT name or list the items — each item's reveal belongs to its own section.

- **Open each section with what's interesting about THIS item** — the surprising claim, the misconception the viewer has been fed, or the specific failure they've probably lived through. Do NOT open with a definition or by announcing the item's name. The item itself can be revealed a sentence or two in, once the hook into it has landed.

- **Explain the fix in plain terms.** Say what's actually going wrong and what to do instead, in language a friend would use across the fence. One sentence of mechanism is fine if that's all it needs; more if the WHY is genuinely interesting. No mandatory length.

- **Sections don't all need the same shape.** Some items want a long explanation, some just need a blunt correction and a specific instruction. Let the item decide. Don't force a teaser bridge into the next section — supporting sections can simply end, and the next one starts. Never speak section numbers aloud in transitions (the hook's cliffhanger and its single callback are the only exceptions).

- **No preamble, no recap, no playbook section.** The first item is the first section after the hook; the last item is the last section before the conclusion.

- **Order for retention.** Open with a strong item, put your single best item second, bury the weakest in the middle, and close on a strong one. The numbered cliffhanger from the hook should be one of the stronger ones placed later.
