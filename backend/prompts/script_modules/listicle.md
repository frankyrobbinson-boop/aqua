- **One segment per item, in the outline's order.** Each item is easily understood to be it's own item
- The hook names one numbered item as the cliffhanger. Read the outline's `Numbered bookmark` element of the hook string and execute it in the hook — "stick with me, because secret #N is the one almost no one gets right" (or equivalent). When the script reaches that section, the section MUST open with a payoff callback (see item-section beat 1).
- **The hook may promise the count and the stakes, but must NOT name or list the items.** Each item's identity is a reveal owned by its own section. "Five fertilizers you have in your kitchen right now" — never "five fertilizers: molasses, banana peels, rice water…" This rule applies to the conclusion too: never recap the list of items by name. The viewer should feel that watching the body was the only way to get the answers. (Note: a compressed-recap drumroll using parallel imperatives is allowed and encouraged — it cadences the lessons, it does not re-name the items.) If you find yourself writing "as we covered, the five are…", delete it.
- Each item-section follows a three-beat internal arc: reframe → mechanism → hard rule. The reframe is the opener (beat 1). The mechanism is the body (beats 2–5). The hard rule is the closer (new beat 6c) — a single declarative/imperative sentence or a two-clause aphorism the viewer should walk away repeating, separate from the open-loop transition ("Lavender wood does not regenerate." / "Treat it like a weed, not a pet."). Keep the arc consistent across every section so the listicle accumulates rhythm; vary the content inside each beat so it never feels templated. The 6–8 beats below fit inside this arc:
  1. Open with a reframe, never a definition. The opener must install this section's micro-loop in one sentence so the viewer leans forward before the technique arrives. Pick one of six shapes: (a) a mindset shift, (b) a confessional question that names the failure the viewer has lived ("if you've ever pruned your lavender and watched it turn into a woody dead-looking donut, raise your hand"), (c) a "here's where most beginners get it backwards" framing, (d) a counterintuitive claim that contradicts the obvious move, (e) a "what if I told you…" reveal, or (f) a direct imperative ("Stop watering it. Almost entirely."). One more allowed-but-rationed shape: a direct-address check-in ("are you ready?") — at most twice across the whole script. Never open with a definition. Never open with the item's name. Never open with logistics ("the next thing we'll talk about is…"). **Banned exact phrasings** (the model has worn them out): "Here's your first mindset shift, and it changes everything…" and "Raise your hand if you've been told to…" — use a different mindset-shift wording or a different shape entirely. **Shape variety across the script.** Each item-section must use a DIFFERENT opening shape from the six above — do not reuse the same shape twice in one script. With 6 shapes and typical lists of 5–10 items, longer lists must lean on every shape; if a list exceeds 6 items, the seventh-onward openers must each repeat a shape AT MOST once and never back-to-back. **If this section is the numbered cliffhanger named in the hook, open with an explicit callback** — "here's the one I promised you," "this is the big one" — BEFORE the reframe.
  2. Reveal the item 1–2 sentences in. The reveal is a payoff. *Exception*: if the section title already names the item (e.g. "SECRET 6: Deadheading…"), the opening sentence should reframe its significance rather than re-announce the name. If this section was forward-referenced in the hook by number, the reveal is a callback — cash the promise explicitly in the line immediately before naming the item: "here's the secret I promised you," "this is the big one I said to stick around for." The callback line and the reveal line are two adjacent sentences; do not separate them with mechanism, analogy, or mistake — those come after.
  3. Mechanism — this is your authority. Explain *why* in physical, biological, or causal terms the viewer can intuit ("the plant thinks mission accomplished and pours its energy into seeds," "wet roots plus cold soil rot the crown"). A felt mechanism replaces a citation — never punt to "experts agree" or "studies show." Show the physical picture before any abstract label. Prefer a triplet here — three stacked sensory phrases or three parallel causes — over a single descriptive sentence; the parallelism is the music. **Mechanism floor — 3 sentences minimum.** Every item-section must spend at least 3 sentences on the physical/biological/causal WHY (the mechanism itself), not on prescription, not on framing, not on the reframe-metaphor. This floor applies to every section — peak (1.4–1.8×) AND supporting (0.7–0.9×) alike; a lean segment is still earned with mechanism, not skipped through. If an item genuinely cannot support 3 sentences of mechanism, replace the item — the section hasn't been earned and the list is weaker for it.
  4. Pair the counterintuitive claim with its mechanism — not a citation. Whenever this item inverts conventional wisdom ("stop watering," "pick the worst spot," "don't fertilize"), the very next sentence must give the physical reason the inversion works ("rich soil makes lavender grow soft and disease-prone," "shallow watering trains roots to stay near the surface"). The mechanism turns the contrarian claim into a felt obvious truth. Named-expert anchors (researcher + year + institution) are *optional and rare* — include only when the research has one and the audience expects that register; default to mechanism. The 3-sentence mechanism floor from beat 3 still applies here — a counterintuitive claim paired with one thin sentence of "because" doesn't clear it.
  5. Reframing metaphor. One arresting metaphor that flips how the viewer categorizes this item — not decoration, but the conceptual spine of the section ("lavender is a tough Mediterranean weed wearing a pretty dress," "roses are solar-powered machines," "fresh stems are flower factories"). The rest of the section can lean on it. Skip only if you genuinely can't find one that reframes — never force a flat comparison, but don't default to skipping either.
  6. Mistake-as-cliffhanger with concession-then-correction. Before naming the corrected behavior, voice the mistaken instinct generously — in the viewer's own words, with their reasoning made to sound understandable ("every instinct as a gardener tells you a plant needs water in summer," "the instinct is to give your rose a quick splash every day, a little drink to keep it happy"). Then pivot — "but lavender's tired look is not thirst," "that instinct is quietly sabotaging you." The pattern: (a) describe the painful, common failure mode vividly enough that the viewer recognizes themselves doing it ("if you've ever watched your X turn into a hollow donut, raise your hand"), (b) pause for a beat, (c) name the fix. The wound must come before the fix; flipping the order kills the suspense. Position the viewer as the *recipient* of bad advice, not its source — "someone told you to," "the instinct is," "you've probably heard" — never "you keep making the mistake of."
  6c. Hard rule before you leave. Drop ONE corrective rule distilled to a beat the viewer can repeat. Allowed shapes are exactly two: (a) a single declarative or imperative sentence ("Lavender wood does not regenerate." / "Treat it like a weed, not a pet." / "Stop watering it."), or (b) a two-clause aphorism — two short balanced beats, often using parallelism or contrast ("Restraint now. Spears forever." / "Rhythm beats randomness."). BANNED: triple-fragment chains of any kind — three stacked fragments like "Water deep. Water rarely. Walk away." or "Stop dividing. Start defending. Watch them thicken." are no longer a sanctioned closer shape; they have become a tell. Where possible, deliver as a hard absolute ("never X," "X does not Y," "X means Z, period") rather than a range or hedge — absolutes read as expertise. Keep it under 10 words. The rule looks backward at this section; the open loop (beat 7) looks forward at the next.
  7. Open loop into the next item. Pick one of three shapes: (a) undo — the next item reverses or outranks this one ("this one feeds the plant — but the next doesn't feed the plant at all"); (b) escalate — the next item raises this one's stakes ("now that your soil is rich, let's talk about feeding — because timing matters as much as the fertilizer itself"); (c) master-key — the next item is the one that decides whether anything else matters ("here's the final secret, and it's the one that determines whether any of the previous four even matter"). Never logistics. Never "next we'll talk about." **Never speak the section number aloud** — no "now for section two," no "section five," no "item three," no "for our next secret, number four." Refer to the upcoming item by its content or its stake, never by its index. (The hook's numbered cliffhanger and its single payoff callback are the only sanctioned places to speak a section number.) Vary the shape across sections so the transitions don't all sound the same.
  8. Aphorism button. Close the item (before the open-loop transition) with one compact, quotable line that distills the lesson into something proverbial — short, balanced, often using parallelism or contrast. Shapes: "A pampered lavender is a boring lavender." "Lavender wants the leftovers." "Rhythm beats randomness." One per item. If you can't write one that earns being remembered, skip — don't force a flat sentence.
- Do not write a recap or "playbook" **segment** — the last item is the last segment. The compressed-recap drumroll lives inside the `conclusion.narration` field only: a single rhythmic sentence or fragment chain that sprints through every item in parallel imperatives, one beat per item, in section order. Each beat must be 3–7 words, same grammatical shape, no elaboration. Example: "Pick the right variety, flood it with morning sun, build living soil, water deep and lazy, mulch heavily, deadhead like you mean it, prune with confidence, feed in rhythm, prevent disease before it starts, work with the seasons." In the conclusion, do not tell the viewer to do all the items — explicitly tell them to pick the ONE that hit hardest and fix just that this week.
- Escalating-stakes ranking inside the body. At least two mid-or-late items must be explicitly labeled as bigger than earlier ones — "this is the big one," "this is the secret that, if you take nothing else from this video, will transform your X," "here's the final secret, and it's the one that determines whether any of the previous matter." Don't let every item feel co-equal; reset the importance gradient at least twice so the middle and the last item both feel like reasons to stay. The numbered-cliffhanger item from the hook must be one of these escalations.
- In the back half, explicitly cross-reference earlier items when the mechanism connects ("this loops right back into secret 3," "now that your soil is rich AND your pruning is sharp..."). Converts a flat list into an interlocking system and rewards the viewer for having watched the whole thing.
- Vary the opening move across items — a question, a "what if," a myth, a tiny story, an imperative ("stop watering it. Almost entirely."). Drop a rhetorical quantifier on the highest-leverage failure mode in 1–2 of the items: "nine times out of ten," "almost no one gets this right," "more than half of buyers walk past the one that actually works." Don't use them in every section — they lose force.
