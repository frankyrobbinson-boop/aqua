## Chapter One — A Root

Before her hands, there is nothing I can tell you. I think roots do not remember the dark the way they remember the first light. She lifted me from a shelf of others like me, turned me over once, and decided.

She carried me in the crook of her arm to a square of bare earth behind a small house. Nothing had been planted there. No flower had stood in that soil before me. She knelt — slowly, even then — and opened the ground with her fingers and set me down into it and pressed the earth back over me, the way you tuck something in for the night.

Then she stood, and looked at the empty bed with me alone in the middle of it, and she did not seem disappointed that I was so small.

I was the first. For a long time I would also be the only.

They say the first year we sleep.

It is true. I did almost nothing. I sent up a few red shoots in spring, thin as wire, and the summer came down on them like a flat hand. The heat pressed my world into stillness. The soil cracked. I learned the particular cruelty of August, which is that it asks everything of you and gives nothing back.

Then winter, which I did not see coming. The cold took my leaves first and then the rest of me, and I went down into the ground believing that this was the end. It was not the end. It was only winter. I would make that mistake many times before I stopped making it.

She came anyway. That is what I remember most of those years — not my struggling, but her coming. She had nothing to look at. I gave her no flower, barely a leaf. Still she came, with a watering can and a small fork, and turned the earth and pulled the weeds from around a plant that was, by any honest account, a poor return on her kindness.

I did not understand patience yet. I only understood that she came.

The second year I crept. A little more of me, a little higher. Still no bloom.

I think now that she was teaching me something without meaning to. That you can be tended before you are worth tending. That someone can believe in the flower while you are still only a root.

## Chapter Two — First Bloom

The third year, I leapt.

I felt it before it showed — a heaviness at the tips of me, a swelling that ached. The buds came up fat and round and shut tight, like fists that did not yet know they were holding anything. For days they did nothing, and I felt her watching them, and I was afraid. She wanted something of me, and I did not know why, but I knew I was disappointing her.

Then one morning, in the cool, I opened.

I will not pretend I can describe it. There was a great deal of me suddenly, where there had been almost nothing — petals upon petals, a weight of white and blush that bent my own stems with its fullness. She came out of the house and stopped. She put one hand to her mouth. She had waited three years, and I had made her wait, and now I gave her everything I had.

It lasted a week.

That is the first thing I learned about myself that frightened me. I had waited three years for a week. By its end my petals browned at the edges and let go, one and then many, and lay on the soil like something spilled. She gathered a few and brought them somewhere I never saw.

A week. I did not know yet that it would always be enough for her, and always be only a week, and that these two truths would have to live inside me, side by side, for a very long time.

## Chapter Three — The Garden

She must have decided that one flower, even a good one, was not a garden. So the bare bed around me began to fill. Bulbs went in along the edge. A rose arrived, balled and wrapped, and was set a few feet from me with great ceremony. And one spring a sunflower came up between us, and grew.

The sunflower grew faster than anything I had ever seen. By summer it stood above all of us, its great face turning to follow the light, and it was certain — that it was the tallest thing that would ever live. It looked down on me from its height. I did not mind. I was busy being what I was.

The first frost took it in a night. I came up to a morning in which it was simply gone, folded down black over its own stalk, and I understood, slowly, that it was not coming back. Not in spring. Not ever. There would be other sunflowers. Not that one.

The annuals were the same. Every year she set out a crowd of them — bright, cheerful, eager — and every year the cold erased them, and the next year a new crowd came that did not know there had been a crowd before. They lived a single season and went, and were replaced by strangers who looked exactly like them.

The rose stayed. For a while I believed I had found another like me, who came back.

I had not yet understood that there are many ways to leave, and that coming back, too, can run out.

## Chapter Four — The Middle

The years began to move differently after that.

My roots grew underground into something that did not fear the winters anymore. Spring after spring I came up surer of myself, bloomed my one week, and went down again — and each time the world around me was a little different.

The rose grew old beside me. I watched it slow, watched its canes thin and its blooms come smaller, and one year it did not bloom at all. It had been the nearest thing I had to my own.

The woman grew old too. Her hands at the soil grew slower, more careful. The fork was set down more often to rest. The watering can she carried got smaller, and then smaller again, until it held hardly anything, and she made more trips with less. A cane appeared, leaning against the fence while she worked. Then it did not lean against the fence anymore. It stayed in her hand.

New flowers came every spring and called the garden theirs. They did not know about the sunflower, or the rose, or the long line of crowds that had stood exactly where they stood.

## Chapter Five — The Last Spring

It was an ordinary day. She was very slow by then. She pulled a few weeds, not all of them. She rested her hand flat on the soil beside me for a while — and then she rose, with effort, and went inside.

The winter came. I went down, as I always did.

In spring I came up, as I always did, and I waited for her the way I had waited every year of my life.

She did not come.

I bloomed anyway. I did not know how to do anything else. I opened into a cloud of white and blush, and there was no one in the doorway. No hand went to a mouth. The week passed. The petals browned and let go and lay on the soil, and no one gathered them, and no one brought them anywhere.

That was how I learned. By blooming into an empty doorway and understanding, at last, what the sunflower had tried to tell me on the first frost of my third year.

There are many ways to leave.

## Chapter Six — Wild Years

After that, the garden was no one's.

The weeds did not ask permission. They came up through the beds and over the edges, tall and indifferent, and there was no fork to turn them under and no hand to pull them. The annuals stopped arriving. The bulbs, untended, dwindled year by year until there were none. The grass moved in from the lawn, and the lawn moved in from the field, and the line between garden and not-garden — faded until there was no line at all.

The fence leaned, and then a part of it fell, and lay where it fell. The house behind me went dark and stayed dark. The garden she had made out of earth and patience went back, slowly, to being just earth.

And every spring, in the middle of it, I bloomed.

I do not entirely know why. There was no one to bloom for. The week I had waited three years for, the week that once stopped her in the doorway with her hand at her mouth — I kept it. In a field that once held a yard, a yard that once held a garden, among weeds that did not look at me, I bloomed, for a week.

## Chapter Seven — A New Place

The clearing began, after how many springs I cannot say. Hands I did not know tore out the weeds, hauled away the fallen fence, opened ground that had closed over itself for years. They came to my bed. A blade went into the soil near my crown. I was, to them, only an old plant in a neglected place.

But it was spring, and I was in bloom.

I do not know what the child said. I only know that the small hands came down close to me, and the cutting stopped, and the voices changed — and instead of being pulled, I was, for the first time in my long life, looked at again.

They did not know about the woman, or the rose, or the sunflower so certain of its height, or the long parade of crowds, or the spring I bloomed into an empty doorway. To them I was only a beginning — an old thing made new and put in fresh soil to see what it would do.

The child lifted me, turned me over once, and decided.

Then carried me under the crook of her arm, the way she did, all those years ago, to a new place.

---

The bed is bare. Nothing has been planted here before me. I am the first, and for a while I will also be the only. I will sleep my first year, and creep my second, and — if I am tended before I am worth tending — leap my third.

I will bloom for a week. It will be enough. It will only ever be a week.

And I will remember. I will remember my first winter, how the cold took my leaves first and then the rest of me, and how I went down into the ground believing it was my end. I will remember my first bloom, how there was a great deal of me, when there had been nothing. I will remember the woman who chose a root off a shelf and believed in a flower that was not yet there. And I will remember —

There are many ways to leave.
