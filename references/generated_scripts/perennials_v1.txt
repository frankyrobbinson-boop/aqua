SEGMENT 1: Give Iris a Sunburn or They Rot (254 words)
visual_notes: Close-up of tan iris rhizomes exposed at soil surface, midday sun glinting; hands brushing mulch away.

Here's your first mindset shift, and it changes everything. Most perennials want their feet tucked under a cozy mulch blanket. Bearded iris wants the opposite. Bearded iris wants its back in the sun. The rhizome — that thick, knobby, horizontal stem sitting at the soil surface — is solar-powered. It photosynthesizes through its tan shoulders. Bury it under three inches of bark mulch like the back of the bag tells you, and you've just trapped moisture against a living stem in the dark. The rhizome turns soft. It smells sour. It rots from the shoulders down, and by July you've got that hollow donut with a sad fan of green at the edges. You've seen it. So here's the rule that actually works. Set the rhizome at or slightly above soil level, so when you press your fingertip on its back at midday it feels warm — sunbaked, like a little loaf of bread. Full sun. Sharp drainage. Twelve to twenty-four inches between plants. Walk out this afternoon and scrape the mulch back with your fingers until you can see tan rhizome again. If you can't see it, you buried it. Then every three to five years, in late summer after bloom, you lift the clump, snap off the firm outer pieces with one leaf fan each, and replant them naked to the sun. That's it. A pampered iris is a rotting iris. Now — section two. This is the big one. The one that saves more beds than anything else on this list.

SEGMENT 2: Wait Two Springs, Eat for Twenty (333 words)
visual_notes: Trench with asparagus crowns being laid deep; later cut to towering summer ferns gone golden in autumn.

Here's the secret I promised you. The one almost nobody gets right. You're going to plant asparagus this spring, and you're going to walk past it for two whole years without picking a single spear. I know that sounds insane. Every instinct in your body says snap one off and taste it. Don't. Here's the mechanism, and once you feel it, you'll never break the rule again. Asparagus is a crown — a fat, octopus-looking bundle of buds and storage roots sitting deep in the soil. Those summer ferns you think look messy? Those are the crown's solar panels. All summer long they pump sugar down into the roots, building the bank account that powers next year's spears. Pick early, and you're not harvesting vegetables. You're withdrawing from a savings account that hasn't been funded yet. Stunted, pencil-thin spears for the rest of the bed's life. So do this. Dig a trench six to eight inches deep. Set crowns twelve to eighteen inches apart, rows four to five feet apart so light reaches every fern. Cover in stages as new growth pushes up the first season. Kneel down and feel the trench depth with your fist — that's how deep. Then walk away. Year three, you take a full harvest. Six to eight weeks of cutting, and the second the spears thin to pencil width — stop. The pencil is the plant telling you the bank is low. Here's the part nobody emphasizes. Do not, under any circumstances, cut those summer ferns down because the bed looks scraggly. Let them stand until they're brown and rattle-dry in fall. That's a properly fed crown talking. Do this once, do it right, and you eat asparagus for fifteen to twenty years from the same trench. That's not a vegetable bed. That's a pension in the ground that pays you every April. Restraint now. Spears forever. Now if shade is the corner that's been defeating you, the next plant fills it without asking for a thing.

SEGMENT 3: Hostas Want Shade, Not Babysitting (244 words)
visual_notes: Dense, heavy hosta mound with broad unperforated leaves in dappled shade; brief shot of slug damage for contrast.

Raise your hand if you've been told to divide your hostas every couple of years. Almost everyone hears that, and almost everyone is being told to do unnecessary work. Here's the truth. The longest-lived, fullest, most jaw-dropping hosta clumps are the ones nobody touched. Hostas spread on short rhizomes with dozens of dormant buds. Leave them in steady moisture, in part to full shade, and they thicken into broad, heavy mounds for decades. Division is optional — only if you want more plants or to shrink one. It is not maintenance. So set the crown right at soil level. Part shade. And here's your finger test — squeeze a handful of soil from right next to the crown. It should hold together like a slice of chocolate cake. If it sifts through your fingers like flour, you've been letting it bake. The real enemies aren't time. They're deer and slugs. Deer strip leaves overnight and starve the roots that feed next year's mound. Slugs punch the lacework you wake up to. Use a physical barrier or repellent spray for deer, and reach for ferric phosphate slug bait — it's pet-safe, it breaks down into iron and phosphate, and it actually works. Stop dividing. Start defending. A hosta you leave alone for ten years looks like furniture. A hosta you keep splitting looks like three sad salads. Next up — a plant that punishes the impatient and rewards the gardener who can wait one season.

SEGMENT 4: Shallow Crown, No Flowers, 20 Years of Pie (255 words)
visual_notes: Hands brushing soil back to reveal pink rhubarb buds at surface; thick red stalks emerging in spring.

If you've ever planted rhubarb, watched it sulk for a year, and yanked a few stalks anyway because the leaves looked big enough — this one's for you. Rhubarb is a crown plant, like asparagus, but it wants the opposite depth. Shallow. On purpose. Set the buds just one to two inches below the soil. Three to four feet between plants. Brush soil back with your fingers until you can just see or feel the knobby pink crown, then cover with a thin veil — not a blanket. Here's why shallow wins. A multi-budded crown sitting near the surface warms up fast in spring and pushes thick, juicy stalks. Bury it deep and it sours. It rots. It sulks for years. Now, the discipline part. Do not harvest the first year. A light picking in year two. Full harvest year three and forever after — twenty-plus years from one crown if you do this right. Here's the part most people skip. The second you see a flower stalk shoot up from the center, snap it off at the base. That flower is a carbohydrate thief. It's siphoning energy straight out of next year's pie. One quick tip — toxic leaves go in the compost or trash, never on your plate, and if a hard frost turns stalks soft and mushy, discard those too. The firm ones are fine. Shallow crown. No flowers. Patient harvest. That's the whole game. Now for the queen. Section five. Plant her once, barely touch her, and she'll outlive your mortgage.

SEGMENT 5: Bury Peony Two Inches, Then Hands Off for 50 Years (369 words)
visual_notes: Hands measuring two finger-widths of soil over pink peony eyes; cut to fully open peony bloom with a single ant on the bud.

This is the anchor. If you take nothing else from this video, take this one. Herbaceous peonies will bloom in the same spot for fifty years or more — fifty — if you plant them right and then, and this is the hard part, leave them completely alone. Here's where almost everyone goes wrong. Someone told you to plant the eyes four to six inches deep, the way you'd plant a bulb. The instinct is to tuck them in safe and snug. That instinct is quietly sabotaging every bloom you'll never see. Buried peonies grow lush leaves and almost no flowers. The eyes — those pink-red buds on the crown — need a kiss of winter chill and a fast spring warm-up. Bury them deep, and the chill never reaches them properly. The plant spends its stored sugar stretching stems through cold soil instead of opening flowers. So here's the rule that actually works. Find the eyes with your fingertips. Set them just one to two inches below the soil surface — knuckle-deep, no more. Measure with two fingers if you have to. Full sun. Do not pile mulch over the crown, ever. That mulch blanket you've been told is good gardening? On a peony, it's a slow smother. Now the myths. You've heard you need ants for the buds to open. You don't. Ants are just sipping sugary nectar — bystanders, freeloaders, harmless. You've heard if it doesn't bloom, dig it up and move it. Don't. Nine times out of ten, the bloom problem is depth, not location. Lift it gently, raise the eyes to two inches, set it back. Walk away. Here's the payoff. Peonies build massive starch-rich storage roots underground — a buried bank vault that gets bigger every single year you don't disturb it. That's why fifty years isn't a brag. It's the default when you stop interfering. Climate gate, because it matters. Peonies need real winter chill. If you garden in a warm zone, pick the coldest microclimate on your property — north side, morning sun, afternoon shade — and you can still pull it off. Plant shallow. Stand back. Let the decades pay you. That, right there, is a pension in the ground.
